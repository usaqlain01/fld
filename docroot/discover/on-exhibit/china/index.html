Many of us think of China as monolithic: a gigantic, singular entity defined by one land, one people, one culture. But China is composed of diverse landscapes, ethnicities, social statuses, and lived experiences that cut across both space and time.

The Field Museum’s Cyrus Tang Hall of China explores this vast country and examines the paradox of constant change and strong continuity that define more than 5,000 years of Chinese cultural history, from the Neolithic period to the present.

Forging Empires:Ritual and Power, War and Unification

Much of what we recognize today as Chinese culture originated during China’s Bronze Age. During this period, rulers expanded control over more territories and peoples, setting a pattern of unification and fragmentation that repeated over succeeding centuries.

Shang Dynasty: circa 1600–1046 BC

Zhou Dynasty: circa 1046–256 BC

Qin Dynasty: 221–206 BC

Even though the dynasty lasted only about 15 years, the Qin [chin] Dynasty gave its name to China and succeeded in uniting the last seven of China’s warring states.

Currency, weights & measures, and writing were standardized under Qin rule.

Han Dynasty: 206 BC–AD 220

While the Roman Empire expanded in the West, the Han Dynasty ruled China and cemented traditions and systems that, in many cases, persisted through China’s imperial period and into modern times.

Six Dynasties: 220–589

Sixteen Kingdoms: 304–438

Sui Dynasty: 589–618

Tang Dynasty: 618–906

China’s territory extended farther than ever before under the Tang Dynasty. New ideas, religions, people, languages, and trade goods poured into China along the “Silk Road” routes.

Five Dynasties & Ten Kingdoms: 907–979

Song Dynasty: 960–1279

Yuan Dynasty: 1279–1368

The Mongols established the Yuan Dynasty in northern China under Kublai Khan in 1271, eventually extending their rule to the south. Yuan rulers adopted Chinese customs and bureaucratic systems.

Ming Dynasty: 1368–1644

Arts and culture flowered during the Ming Dynasty, and trade with Europe made Chinese ceramics famous in the West.

Qing Dynasty: 1644–1911

China’s last dynasty, the Qing [ching], ruled during a long period of stability and expansion. However, events of the 19th and 20th centuries brought wrenching change, bringing the dynastic system to its end in 1911.

Republic of China: 1912–1949

People's Republic of China: 1949-Present

Eight spirit stones, a gift to The Field Museum from the Municipal Government of Suzhou, China, serve as centerpieces for the garden space at the end of the Cyrus Tang Hall of China.
